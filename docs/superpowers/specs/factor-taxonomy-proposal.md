**sw-demand-depth** — General software-engineer demand & market depth [w22, kept-reframed]

> RESEARCH (gov-first, BD-relevant): official labour-shortage / bottleneck-occupation lists (e.g. Germany Bitkom + Fachkräftemangel lists, UK Immigration Salary List, Australia Skills Priority List / Core Skills Occupation List, Canada NOC TEER + Express Entry category draws, NL/IND tech shortage stats), national-statistics-office ICT vacancy & employment data, OECD/Eurostat ICT-specialist employment growth, and whether 'software developer / programmer' sits on the official shortage occupation list (which directly eases sponsorship). Cross-check with current 2025-26 tech-hiring sentiment (post-layoff recovery, junior-vs-senior balance) from reputable labour reports, not blogs. SCORING anchors: ~90 = software dev formally on the national shortage/priority list, large absorptive market (tens of thousands of unfilled roles or very high vacancy rate), multiple English-speaking tech hubs, healthy hiring for sub-senior engineers. ~50 = real but moderate demand, software not specially privileged on shortage lists, market 'pickier' favouring experienced hires, limited hubs. ~20 = small/saturated market, no shortage-list status, weak vacancy growth or active contraction, juniors struggle to place.

_Rationale:_ Core driver of the category and of whether the couple can land in-country roles at all; reframed to tie demand to official shortage-list status, which mechanically eases sponsorship.

**ai-ml-specialisation** — AI / ML specialisation depth [w14, new]

> RESEARCH: the couple's differentiating skill is AI/software, so assess the country's AI ecosystem specifically — government AI strategy & funding (national AI plans, OECD AI Policy Observatory), concentration of AI/ML/data-science roles in official bottleneck lists, presence of AI research labs and AI-heavy employers/startups (Stanford AI Index country rankings for talent/investment, named hubs), and named AI/ML demand in tech-vacancy reports. Distinguish from generic IT demand: a country can have IT shortages but shallow AI depth, or be an AI magnet (US, UK, parts of EU). SCORING anchors: ~90 = top-tier AI hub, large private AI investment, AI/ML explicitly among most-in-demand roles, dense lab/employer presence absorbing two AI engineers. ~50 = growing AI sector concentrated in 1-2 cities, AI demand present but not a national priority, most roles are conventional software. ~20 = thin AI scene, little public AI investment, AI roles scarce or confined to a single firm/region.

_Rationale:_ Profession priority is 'IT / Software / AI'; AI depth diverges sharply from generic SW demand across the 20 countries and is the couple's competitive edge.

**salary-levels** — Software / AI salary levels (purchasing-power aware) [w16, kept]

> RESEARCH: median/typical gross salaries for software engineers and AI/ML/data roles from authoritative sources (national statistics offices, OECD average-wage data, official labour-market salary bands, plus reputable salary surveys like levels.fyi/Stack Overflow cross-checked against gov wage data). Note entry-to-mid bands (post-MSc reality), not just senior. Where relevant flag whether salary clears visa/PR salary thresholds (links to hireability and PR). Keep this about gross pay scale and currency of figures; net-vs-cost lives in income-cost. SCORING anchors: ~90 = high engineer salaries in absolute and PPP terms (e.g. US/CH/top hubs), strong premium for AI/ML. ~50 = solid European-average tech pay, comfortable but not exceptional, modest AI premium. ~20 = low tech wages relative to peer destinations, weak or no specialist premium.

_Rationale:_ Pay drives the value of relocating and feeds PR salary thresholds; central to the category but bounded so it does not double-count cost-of-living.

**sponsorship-work-authorisation** — Employer sponsorship & work-authorisation friction [w20, kept-reframed]

> RESEARCH (gov-first): how a foreign post-study worker actually gets authorised to work — official skilled-work / work-permit routes (e.g. EU Blue Card thresholds, UK Skilled Worker + licensed-sponsor register & salary floor, Australia employer-sponsored/skilled streams, Canada LMIA vs LMIA-exempt + PGWP-to-PR), whether a resident-labour-market test applies, salary thresholds gating sponsorship, employer licensing burden, and whether software/AI is a regulated occupation (it generally is not, but verify). Assess how willing/able typical employers are to sponsor non-EU/non-citizen hires and the volume of sponsored tech visas issued. SCORING anchors: ~90 = no labour-market test for shortage tech roles, low/clear salary threshold, simple Blue-Card-style or post-study-to-work conversion, employers routinely sponsor, high sponsored-visa volume. ~50 = sponsorship available but with salary thresholds, some employer reluctance, paperwork friction, or partial labour-market testing. ~20 = strict labour-market test, high salary floors, few licensed sponsors, employers avoid sponsoring non-citizens, low issuance.

_Rationale:_ For a study->work->PR pathway, the legal ability to convert a job offer into authorised employment is decisive; reframed from 'norms' to the concrete thresholds/tests that gate it.

**foreign-grad-bd-hireability** — Foreign-graduate & BD-applicant real hireability [w12, new]

> RESEARCH (BD lens, real-world): beyond legal demand, can two Bangladeshi post-MSc graduates actually get hired? Look for evidence on: language-gating of tech jobs (English-workable vs local-language-required engineering roles), employer attitudes to non-EU/foreign hires and to candidates with BD/online-remote experience, recognition/parity of foreign degrees & prior experience, junior-vs-experienced saturation for fresh post-MSc graduates, and outcomes data for international graduates staying and finding skilled work (OECD migrant-employment-gap data, official graduate-retention/stay-rate stats, BD diaspora tech employment anecdote cross-checked with data). Distinguish high legal demand but practical barriers (e.g. German-only roles, junior glut). SCORING anchors: ~90 = English-workable tech sector, strong international-graduate stay/retention, employers value foreign + remote experience, low migrant employment gap, fresh post-MSc grads place well. ~50 = English roles exist mainly in hubs/MNCs, some local-language and experience-recognition barriers, moderate migrant employment gap. ~20 = most roles need fluent local language, weak recognition of foreign/online experience, large migrant employment gap, poor international-graduate retention.

_Rationale:_ Legal demand overstates outcomes for BD post-MSc grads; language-gating, experience recognition and migrant employment gaps are the decisive real-world filter for this nationality/profile.

**dual-earner-depth** — Dual-earner market depth (both spouses placed) [w8, kept-reframed]

> RESEARCH: both intend to work, so assess whether the market can absorb TWO software/AI engineers, ideally co-located. Look for: number and geographic spread of tech hubs (can both find roles without splitting cities), breadth of employers (avoid single-employer-town risk), and whether the market depth supports a trailing spouse finding a role of similar level. Use vacancy counts, hub diversity, and employer-concentration signals from official/statistics sources. SCORING anchors: ~90 = multiple deep tech hubs, thousands of concurrent roles, both spouses can realistically be placed in the same metro at comparable levels. ~50 = one or two real hubs, both employable but with some geographic or level compromise. ~20 = thin/single-hub market where placing one is hard and placing both together is unlikely.

_Rationale:_ Profile is a dual-income couple relocating together; reframed from generic 'market size' to the specific question of absorbing two co-located tech earners.

**remote-acceptance** — Remote-work acceptance [w0, REMOVED]

> REMOVED.

_Rationale:_ Goal is physical in-country residence accruing toward PR/citizenship; remote/offshore work builds neither residency nor sponsorship, and any fallback-income value belongs in income-cost, not this category.

**other** — Other / category-specific findings [w8, new]

> USE: catch-all for material job-market findings that do not fit the named factors but affect this profile's ability to build a tech career toward PR/citizenship. Examples: acute 2025-26 tech-layoff or hiring-freeze cycles, heavy sector/employer concentration risk (one dominant firm or industry), startup/tech-founder or scale-up visa schemes that ease entry, gig/contracting norms for engineers, AI-driven displacement of junior software roles, or unusually strong/weak international-tech-recruitment pipelines from South Asia. SCORING: default to ~50 (neutral, no notable extra signal). Raise toward ~80-90 for a clearly favourable structural advantage well-evidenced from authoritative sources; lower toward ~20 for a well-evidenced material headwind (e.g. ongoing mass tech layoffs, single-employer dependence). Only deviate from neutral with a cited authoritative source; record what was found in the cell reasoning.

_Rationale:_ Moderate 'other' weight: the tech market is broad and volatile across 20 countries (layoff cycles, AI disruption, startup-visa schemes) so a meaningful catch-all is warranted, but the named factors already cover the core determinants.

**weightSum: 100**

**notes:** Redesigned around the actual decision for two post-MSc engineer spouses who must BE HIRED in-country to reach PR->citizenship. Kept demand (split into general SW demand vs AI/ML specialisation depth, since the couple's edge is AI and these diverge by country), kept salary, kept dual-spouse market depth (reframed to absorption of TWO tech earners), and kept sponsorship (reframed to the full hireability gate: thresholds, labour-market tests, regulated-occupation friction, work-permit ease). NEW: 'Foreign-graduate / BD-applicant hireability' to capture the real-world barriers the Germany cell already hints at (junior-saturated markets, language-gated roles, recognition of online/BD experience, bias against non-EU hires) — the single biggest blind spot in the old set for this lens. REMOVED 'Remote acceptance': the goal is physical residence accruing toward PR and citizenship; remote/offshore work neither builds residency nor sponsorship, so rewarding it pulls scores in the wrong direction (counterargument: remote could be a fallback income bridge, but that belongs in income-cost, not here). 'other' set to 8 (moderate breadth/volatility: tech-layoff cycles, sector concentration risk, startup-visa schemes, employer concentration vary a lot across 20 countries and 2026 conditions shift fast).

**approval-refusal-rate** — BD student-visa approval vs refusal rate [w22, kept-reframed]

> RESEARCH (gov-first, BD-specific): find the study/student-visa approval (or refusal) rate for BANGLADESHI nationals to this country. Sources in priority order: (a) the destination's embassy/high-commission in Dhaka (FAQ, statistics pages); (b) the destination's national immigration/visa authority published refusal-rate or grant-rate statistics broken down by nationality (e.g. UK Home Office immigration statistics by nationality, US DoS B/F-visa refusal-rate tables, EU Schengen short-stay refusal rates as a proxy where study-specific data is absent — flag the proxy); (c) reputable Bangladeshi reporting (Dhaka Tribune, The Daily Star, Prothom Alo) citing official figures. Distinguish STUDENT visa rate from general/short-stay rate; the student rate governs this profile. Cross-check >=2 independent authoritative sources; prefer the most recent official figure. SCORING 0-100 (higher = easier to get a yes): ~90 = published BD student-visa approval >=90% with no nationality-specific penalty (e.g. Germany-Dhaka ~90%); ~65 = approval roughly 70-85% or a moderate nationality risk-flag; ~50 = approval ~50-65%, BD treated as elevated-risk nationality, meaningful refusal likelihood; ~20 = refusal rate >50% / BD on a high-refusal list / routine denials for this visa class; <=10 = effectively closed or near-blanket refusal for BD nationals. If only a short-stay proxy exists, cap confidence and note it in reasoning.

_Rationale:_ The single most decisive thing for this profile is whether a Bangladeshi gets the yes; reframed to the student-visa rate specifically, not generic acceptance.

**dhaka-mission-appointment-capacity** — Dhaka mission access & appointment capacity [w20, merged]

> RESEARCH (gov-first, BD-specific): (1) Is the study visa lodged AND DECIDED in Dhaka, or must the applicant travel/route through a third country (New Delhi, Bangkok, Islamabad) or a non-resident hub? (2) What is the realistic APPOINTMENT/SLOT availability and backlog for BD applicants — booking-to-appointment wait, not in-queue processing. Sources: the destination embassy/high-commission Dhaka site, VFS Global / TLScontact Dhaka pages, and BD media reporting on slot waits and demand-vs-capacity (e.g. embassy clearing ~2,000/yr against ~80,000 demand => multi-month/year waits). Capture annual processing capacity vs demand where reported. SCORING 0-100: ~90 = full application + decision in Dhaka, slots readily available within ~2-4 weeks, no third-country travel; ~65 = Dhaka-based but routine waits of ~1-3 months, or VFS biometrics only with embassy retaining the decision locally; ~50 = Dhaka mission exists but severe slot backlog (~6-12 months) OR partial third-country dependency; ~20 = must travel to a third country for biometrics/decision, or appointment backlog ~12-19+ months (e.g. Germany-Dhaka reported up to ~19 months); <=10 = no Dhaka mission and mandatory long-haul travel plus heavy backlog. Treat a long slot backlog as nearly as disqualifying as a high refusal rate.

_Rationale:_ Merges 'embassy in Dhaka vs travel' with slot/backlog capacity — physical presence is moot if slots are a year out; the slot bottleneck is often the true binding constraint.

**in-queue-processing-schemes** — In-queue processing time, complexity & special schemes [w13, kept-reframed]

> RESEARCH (gov-first): once an application is LODGED, how long and how complex is adjudication, and what mandatory pre-screening schemes apply to BD applicants? Capture: (a) official decision time for a lodged student visa (embassy SLA / immigration-authority service standard); (b) availability of priority/super-priority/fast-track paid options; (c) mandatory pre-checks specific to nationality — e.g. APS academic-evaluation certificate (note: NOT required for Bangladesh by Germany, but IS for India/China/Vietnam — country-specific), ATAS, biometrics, credibility pre-checks, or VFS routing layers that add steps. Sources: destination immigration authority service-standard pages, embassy Dhaka FAQ, APS/ATAS official portals. SCORING 0-100 (higher = faster/simpler): ~90 = lodged decision in <=3-4 weeks, simple checklist, no mandatory pre-scheme, optional priority available; ~65 = ~1-2 month decision, standard document set, one extra scheme (e.g. ATAS) but manageable; ~50 = ~2-3 month decision OR a mandatory pre-screening scheme (APS-type) adding weeks and cost; ~20 = 3-6+ month adjudication, multiple stacked schemes/agencies, opaque or frequently-delayed processing; <=10 = unpredictable multi-month-plus processing with high RFE/admin-processing rates. Keep this distinct from appointment-slot wait (scored in the Dhaka-access factor).

_Rationale:_ Reframed to post-lodgement processing plus mandatory schemes (APS/ATAS/priority), de-duplicated from the appointment wait now in factor 2.

**financial-bars** — Financial-proof / blocked-account bars [w13, kept]

> RESEARCH (gov-first, BD-relevant): the up-front financial bar a Dhaka applicant must clear and how hard it is to satisfy from Bangladesh. Capture: (a) blocked-account / proof-of-funds amount and mechanism (e.g. Germany blocked account ~EUR 11,904/yr; UK maintenance funds held 28 days; Australia genuine-funds threshold); (b) whether funds must sit for a minimum period and in approved instruments; (c) cross-border transfer friction from Bangladesh — Bangladesh Bank student-remittance limits, endorsement requirements, and whether sponsors' funds are accepted. Sources: destination immigration authority funds rules, embassy Dhaka FAQ, Bangladesh Bank student-remittance circulars. Convert all amounts via formatters (en-GB) and express relative to BD median income for context. SCORING 0-100 (higher = lower/easier bar): ~90 = modest, flexible proof of funds, sponsor funds accepted, no long lock-in, transferable from BD without friction; ~65 = moderate amount with a short holding period, standard but achievable; ~50 = sizeable blocked-account/lock-in (e.g. ~EUR 11-12k held for a year) that is a real but surmountable hurdle for a middle-class Dhaka family; ~20 = very high funds bar plus rigid instruments and heavy BD-side remittance friction; <=10 = funds bar effectively out of reach for typical applicants or transfer is blocked. Flag any audit-trail/genuineness rule on source-of-funds here only as it affects the AMOUNT/mechanism; intent-genuineness goes in the scrutiny factor.

_Rationale:_ Blocked-account/maintenance bars are a hard, BD-specific financial gate (with remittance friction) that independently sinks otherwise-eligible applicants.

**credibility-genuineness-scrutiny** — Credibility & genuineness scrutiny [w12, kept-reframed]

> RESEARCH (gov-first, BD-specific): how heavily are BD applicants scrutinised on GENUINENESS — genuine student / genuine temporary entrant intent, credibility interviews, and source-of-funds authenticity — beyond the headline approval number. Capture: (a) presence and rigour of a genuineness test (e.g. Australia Genuine Student requirement, UK credibility interviews, US 214(b) non-immigrant-intent burden, NZ genuine-intent) and whether BD nationals are routinely flagged; (b) document-verification burden (notarisation, attestation, third-party verification of BD academic/financial docs, fraud-prevention crackdowns targeting BD); (c) interview likelihood and refusal reasons cited for BD. Sources: destination immigration policy/caseworker guidance, embassy Dhaka document checklists, BD media on document-fraud crackdowns and interview refusals. SCORING 0-100 (higher = lighter, more predictable scrutiny): ~90 = light, checklist-based, no interview, BD docs accepted with standard attestation, low refusal-for-credibility risk; ~65 = standard genuineness test that well-prepared genuine applicants clear comfortably; ~50 = active genuineness/credibility regime where BD applicants face notable refusal risk on intent or funds-genuineness and elevated document verification; ~20 = aggressive scrutiny, common credibility refusals for BD, interview lottery, heavy attestation/verification burden; <=10 = pervasive suspicion of BD nationals driving refusals regardless of merit. Do not double-count the raw approval rate (factor 1) — score the PROCESS burden and intent-risk here.

_Rationale:_ Reframes the old 'document scrutiny burden' into the genuine-student/intent + funds-genuineness lens that is the actual BD refusal driver, kept distinct from headline rate.

**heightened-scrutiny-policy-risk** — Heightened-scrutiny / policy-risk trend for BD nationals [w12, kept-reframed]

> RESEARCH (gov-first + reputable news, BD-specific, forward-looking): recent and current measures that single out or disadvantage Bangladeshi nationals, and the trend. Capture: (a) explicit bans, suspensions, visa pauses, or country caps affecting BD (e.g. US travel-ban watchlists, embassy intake suspensions); (b) nationality-specific risk profiling, fraud-driven crackdowns, or sponsor/institution blacklists hitting BD students; (c) directionality — tightening vs easing over the last ~12 months, and any announced changes for 2025-26. Sources: destination immigration-authority announcements, government press releases, embassy Dhaka notices, OECD/UNHCR and reputable BD media. Confirm currency (<=12 months); flag and replace anything older. SCORING 0-100 (higher = lower policy risk / stable-or-easing): ~90 = no BD-specific adverse measures, stable or actively easing policy, BD treated as a normal source country; ~65 = generally stable with minor friction and no targeting trend; ~50 = some heightened scrutiny or tightening signals for BD, watch-listed but no hard ban; ~20 = recent BD-specific clampdown, intake pause, or strong tightening trajectory; <=10 = active ban, suspension, or near-blanket heightened scrutiny on BD nationals. Record any disagreement between sources and the date of the latest measure in the cell reasoning.

_Rationale:_ Kept but made forward-looking and BD-specific — policy volatility (bans, pauses, crackdowns) can flip accessibility overnight and must be tracked as a trend.

**other** — Other / category-specific findings [w8, new]

> CATCH-ALL for material, authoritatively-sourced findings about BD-applicant visa accessibility that do not fit the six named factors. Examples: agent/broker dependency and scam exposure in the Dhaka market; biometrics/passport-validity or police-clearance peculiarities; reciprocal or bilateral arrangements (scholarships-linked visa lanes, MoUs) that ease BD access; courier/passport-return logistics from a third-country hub; sudden fee shocks; or institution-specific sponsor licensing that gates BD students. USE: only record a finding here when it materially moves accessibility for THIS profile and is backed by a gov-first or reputable source with a date; if a finding actually belongs to a named factor, score it there instead. SCORING 0-100: default to ~65 (neutral) when nothing material is found, so 'other' neither rewards nor penalises absent evidence; move toward ~90 for a discovered facilitating factor (e.g. a BD-specific scholarship visa lane), and toward ~20-30 for a discovered structural obstacle (e.g. mandatory exploitative agent routing, frequent passport-courier loss). State the specific finding and citation in the cell reasoning; if genuinely nothing material, mark neutral and say so.

_Rationale:_ Operationally broad, fast-churning category for BD nationals; a mid-high 8 leaves headroom for real-world frictions (agents, logistics, MoUs) not captured by named factors.

**time-to-citizenship** — Time to citizenship [w26, kept-reframed]

> RESEARCH (gov-first): on the destination's citizenship/naturalisation authority site (e.g. UK Home Office, BAMF/Germany, IRCC/Canada, Norway UDI), find the standard ordinary-residence period required before a naturalisation application can be filed, measured from arrival on the study/work route this couple would actually use. Confirm: (a) whether time spent on a student visa counts toward the residency clock (often it does NOT or only partly counts), (b) whether PR/settlement time is a prerequisite and how it stacks on top, (c) any post-PR waiting period, and (d) that BOTH spouses qualify independently (no derivative-only path that leaves one behind). Cross-check the as-built clock against ≥2 official sources because reforms move fast. SCORING anchors (lower total realistic years from arrival = higher): ~90 = citizenship realistically attainable in ≤5 years from arrival with student time counting; ~50 = ~7-9 years total (typical EU: study often excluded, then PR, then 5-yr naturalisation); ~20 = ≥10 years, study time excluded, long PR-then-citizenship stacking, or only one spouse can realistically qualify on the standard route. This is the dominant driver of the category for a couple whose explicit goal endpoint is the passport.

_Rationale:_ Time-to-passport is the profile's stated endpoint and fasterCitizenship=true; reframed to count from arrival, verify study-time inclusion and PR stacking, and require both spouses qualify.

**dual-citizenship-retention** — Dual citizenship (both sides) [w13, kept-reframed]

> RESEARCH two-sided (BD lens is essential here): (a) Destination side — does the country permit naturalised citizens to retain a prior nationality, or require renunciation? Check the citizenship authority and any reservations. (b) Bangladesh side — Bangladesh permits dual nationality only with a defined list of countries via a Dual Nationality Certificate (DNC) issued by the Home Ministry/Bangladesh missions; verify (Bangladesh Citizenship Act / DNC notifications, Bangladesh High Commission pages) whether the SPECIFIC destination is on the permitted list and whether a DNC is straightforward to obtain. A country allowing dual citizenship is worthless to this couple if Bangladesh would treat acquisition as automatic loss of Bangladeshi nationality. SCORING anchors: ~90 = destination allows dual AND Bangladesh permits retention/DNC for that country, so they keep both passports cleanly; ~50 = one side imposes friction (e.g. destination allows it but Bangladesh requires a discretionary DNC, or a renunciation that is reversible) or status is ambiguous in current sources; ~20 = renunciation of Bangladeshi nationality effectively required, or Bangladesh does not permit dual nationality with this country. dualCitizenship is a stated profile preference.

_Rationale:_ dualCitizenship=preferred; made nationality-specific (Bangladesh's DNC regime is country-restricted), not the generic 'does the country allow it' the old factor implied.

**naturalisation-requirements** — Naturalisation requirements (language, civics, integration) [w22, kept-reframed]

> RESEARCH (gov-first): from the naturalisation authority, enumerate the on-paper qualifying conditions beyond mere time: required language level (e.g. CEFR B1/B2) and which language, civics/'life in country' test, minimum income / no-reliance-on-benefits / continuous-employment conditions, physical-presence days, and good-character requirements. Assess difficulty for two English-speaking software engineers whose native language is Bangla (English-medium destinations are far easier; B2 in German/Dutch/Norwegian/Finnish is a real multi-year burden borne by BOTH spouses). SCORING anchors (lower/easier bar = higher): ~90 = English-language country, light/no separate language exam, simple civics test, income easily met by SW salaries (e.g. Ireland/UK/Australia/NZ/Canada profile); ~50 = B1 plus a civics test in a non-English language that is achievable but demands sustained study by both; ~20 = B2+ in a hard non-English language, demanding integration/income tests, or onerous continuous-presence rules that the couple's mobility could break. Score the harder-positioned spouse, since both must naturalise.

_Rationale:_ Split the old 'residency/language' factor: this captures the qualitative on-paper bar (the residency clock moved to its own factor); language is a major real cost for two Bangla-native applicants in non-English states.

**citizenship-attainability** — Attainability in practice (cost, discretion, security) [w11, new]

> RESEARCH (gov-first + reputable reporting): beyond eligibility on paper, how attainable and secure is naturalisation in practice? Check: application/test fees and biometrics costs for two applicants; processing backlogs/typical decision times published by the authority; whether grant is a right once criteria are met or remains discretionary; documented refusal rates or recent tightening; and revocation/loss risk for naturalised citizens (some states can strip naturalised citizenship more readily). BD lens: note any extra documentation/legalisation burden routed through Dhaka or a regional mission. - **post-study-work:** dual-graduate-spouse-psw (both spouses' independent PSW rights) overlaps spouse-family's spouse-work-rights and spouse-path-to-independence. A spouse who is also a graduate getting their own PSW is the same fact as the accompanying spouse's route to independent status, double-counting the dual-engineer-couple advantage. — _Fix:_ Disambiguate by scope: keep dual-graduate-spouse-psw for the case where BOTH partners studied and each earns a PSW in their own right (graduate-to-graduate); restrict spouse-family factors to the DEPENDANT/accompanying case (one studies, one accompanies). - **spouse-family:** spouse-work-rights, dependent-visa-bd-access, and spouse-path-to-independence re-measure the same dual-earner placement story that job-market dual-earner-depth and post-study-work dual-graduate-spouse-psw also cover. dual-career-feasibility (w6) is near-identical in intent to job-market dual-earner-depth (w8). Risk of triple-counting 'both spouses can work'. — _Fix:_ Split cleanly: (a) LEGAL right to work / visa access = spouse-family (spouse-work-rights, dependent-visa-bd-access); (b) MARKET ability to place two engineers = job-market dual-earner-depth ONLY; (c) PSW-phase independent permits = post-study-work. Remove dual-career-feasibility from spouse-family (it is the job-market concept) and fold its 6 points into family-reunification-children or spouse-path-to-independence.

- **spouse-family:** 'other' is weighted w16, equal to family-reunification-children and higher than two real factors (spouse-path-to-independence w14, dual-career-feasibility w6). A catch-all at 16% is disproportionate and signals the set is under-specified - 'other' should be a small residual. — _Fix:_ Reduce other to ~6-8 and reallocate the freed ~8-10 points to the load-bearing factors (spouse-work-rights, dependent-visa-bd-access are decisive for this couple). If dual-career-feasibility is removed per the prior fix, redistribute there too.

- **healthcare:** social-insurance-safety-net (unemployment, sickness & pension) is welfare/income-security, not healthcare, and its parental/maternity element overlaps family-childcare-benefits (an income transfer counted twice). — _Fix:_ Keep social-insurance-safety-net in healthcare (the category is Healthcare & Welfare) but narrow family-childcare-benefits to childcare ACCESS/availability and put parental/maternity CASH benefit under social-insurance-safety-net to remove the maternity double-count. - **politics:** targeted-risk-south-asian-muslim (w10) overlaps safety-law lived-discrimination-hatecrime, muslim-diaspora sentiment-safety, and culture-language social-acceptance-south-asian; anti-immigrant sentiment is also already in politics' own societal-sentiment-trend and anti-immigration-electoral-power (internal + cross-category redundancy). — _Fix:_ Disambiguate by mechanism: politics = STATE/policy-level targeting (laws, party platforms, electoral threat aimed at South-Asian/Muslim migrants); safety-law = LIVED hate-crime/discrimination and enforcement; muslim-diaspora = day-to-day religious sentiment/safety. Reword targeted-risk-south-asian-muslim to 'policy/electoral targeting' so it does not re-score lived hate-crime.

- **politics:** Internal redundancy: societal-sentiment-trend (w13) and anti-immigration-electoral-power (w16) both capture the anti-immigrant climate; immigration-policy-volatility (w26) and non-eu-skilled-policy-direction (w18) both capture migrant policy trajectory. Four factors split two underlying signals, diluting each. — _Fix:_ Either merge societal-sentiment-trend into anti-immigration-electoral-power (sentiment leads electoral power) or, if kept separate, define societal-sentiment-trend as grassroots/poll sentiment and electoral-power as actual seat/coalition share with no shared evidence. Same treatment for the two policy-direction factors.

- **tax:** household-dual-earner-treatment (w18) overlaps income-cost household-net-surplus (w30) - both evaluate the two-engineer household's net financial position, so the dual-earner advantage is counted twice. — _Fix:_ Scope tax to RATE/WEDGE mechanics only: household-dual-earner-treatment should measure whether the tax SYSTEM penalises or favours two earners (joint vs individual assessment, transferable allowances), NOT the resulting cash. income-cost owns the cash net-surplus outcome. - **income-cost:** student-phase-affordability (w14) and upfront-relocation-cash (w10) overlap msc-study affordability-funding (w26) and tax student-phase-tax-exposure, and the proof-of-funds cash overlaps visa-access financial-bars (w13). A high score here mechanically implies high PSW/PR scores, double-counting the pipeline. — _Fix:_ Either remove study-pathway-fit and let post-study-work/pr-pathway carry downstream enablement, or narrow it strictly to STUDY-PROGRAM attributes affecting eligibility (e.g. degree level/length meeting the PSW minimum) without re-scoring PSW/PR generosity, which is owned downstream.

Four 'is the rule stable' factors across four categories risk rewarding/penalising stable-policy countries four times. — _Fix:_ Keep each stability factor narrowly scoped to its own stage (citizenship-rule vs PR-rule vs PSW-rule) and treat politics' immigration-policy-volatility as the general cross-cutting signal. ### crossCutting (verbatim)

Five concepts recur across categories and need an explicit single-owner rule documented in each factor description so reviewers score each fact once. (1) DUAL-EARNER / BOTH-SPOUSES-WORK: job-market dual-earner-depth, spouse-family (spouse-work-rights, spouse-path-to-independence, dual-career-feasibility), post-study-work dual-graduate-spouse-psw, tax household-dual-earner-treatment. Owner split: legal right to work + dependant-visa access = spouse-family; market capacity to place two engineers = job-market; PSW-phase independent permits = post-study-work; tax-system treatment of two earners = tax (system only, not cash); resulting cash = income-cost. (2) POLICY/RULE STABILITY: per-stage in citizenship policy-stability, pr-pathway policy-predictability, post-study-work psw-policy-stability, plus macro politics immigration-policy-volatility + non-eu-skilled-policy-direction. Owner split: each stage-factor = recent rule changes for THAT stage only; politics = macro cross-stage volatility trend. (3) ANTI-MIGRANT / SOUTH-ASIAN-MUSLIM SENTIMENT: safety-law lived-discrimination-hatecrime, muslim-diaspora sentiment-safety, culture-language social-acceptance-south-asian, politics (societal-sentiment-trend, targeted-risk-south-asian-muslim). Owner split: state/electoral targeting = politics; lived hate-crime + enforcement = safety-law; day-to-day religious sentiment = muslim-diaspora; general social-acceptance attitude = culture-language. (4) STUDENT-PHASE MONEY: msc-study affordability-funding, income-cost (student-phase-affordability, upfront-relocation-cash), tax student-phase-tax-exposure, visa-access financial-bars. Owner split: tuition+scholarships = msc-study; living cost+relocation cash = income-cost; tax during study = tax; regulatory proof-of-funds threshold as a visa barrier = visa-access. (5) STATUS-LOSS / RETENTION RISK: pr-pathway continuity-retention, safety-law immigration-enforcement-due-process, politics volatility. Owner split: probability of losing status to rule change = pr-pathway/politics; quality of enforcement conduct and due process = safety-law.

### overall (verbatim)

Structurally the taxonomy is close: 13 of 14 categories sum to exactly 100 and every category has an 'other' residual. Two mechanical defects: culture-language sums to 106 (must lose 6) and job-market still carries remote-acceptance as a dead [w0, removed] entry that should be deleted, not retained. The substantive risk is systematic double-counting along five recurring axes - dual-earner ability, policy/rule stability, anti-migrant sentiment, student-phase money, and status-loss risk - each scored in 3-4 categories. These do not break the weight math (categories are independently weighted) but they will bias overall scores toward countries strong (or weak) on those axes because the same underlying fact is rewarded several times in the weighted sum. Fix by writing an explicit single-owner boundary into each affected factor's description (per the cross-cutting map) rather than deleting factors, since stage-specific framing is genuinely useful; the only outright removals worth considering are spouse-family dual-career-feasibility (duplicate of job-market dual-earner-depth) and msc-study study-pathway-fit (duplicate of downstream PSW/PR enablement). Also rebalance spouse-family's oversized 'other' (w16). Per the data-change protocol none of these weight or factor edits should ship without the deep-research step and the Zod test gate, and any weight change must be re-validated to keep every category at 100.
